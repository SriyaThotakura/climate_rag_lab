© This informed what is confidential. It is up to members to disclose what they want
to.
e Crucial advice from this presentation is to ask for advice before the act or vote in
question
e Information on voting
o Something to remember when thinking about conflicts of interest:
Disclose/discuss/don’t vote
® Disclose conflicts before discussions or before votes
® Disclose/Discuss/Don’t vote on things that directly impact or touch you
o Examples provided and the factors to consider:
* Liquor licenses: License for own business vs for a neighboring business
* Sitting on a nonprofit board: Sitting on a board vs being a dues-paying
member
® Working for a city agency/working for the city: Being primarily employed
® Chairing a committee: Can sit on a committee, but cannot chair a
committee where you need to disclose, discuss, don’t vote
o Vote tabulation: To pass a vote on an issue, a majority of yeses is needed. The
total count would be the total of people eligible to vote.
o Seeking personal favors or favorable treatment. Saying “Do you know who I
am?”
= Don’t.
o City business vs personal business: Don’t use seals, letterhead, etc. for personal
business
o Gifts: Don’t take gifts from people trying to gift to you because of who you
are/wanting to sway your opinion.
« Example: accepting a gift or getting your bill paid by a restaurant
© Not okay: if voting on their liquor license
e Okay: a waiter offering you a free dessert at the end of a meal you
paid for (they don’t know who you are)
o Don’t try to use Community Board sway in representing your firm
o Dilletta: Regarding making a complaint. What is your responsibility if you know
someone may have committed a violation?
* Can make confidential complaints. Once you complain, it gets handled,
but any findings are not fed back to you.
o Bakary: Scenario: someone with a restaurant. A competitor, a known enemy next
door, is applying for a liquor license.
= Yes, can vote.
o Juan: Does this meet board members’ training requirement for conflict of interest?
* Yes, can be used in place of attending a training (as long as present at this
meeting).
